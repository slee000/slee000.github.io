Triboresistive Touch Sensing: Grid‐Free Touch Point Recognition Based on Monolayered Ionic Power Generators


Advanced Materials, 2022.

In this study, I set up a system to demonstrate that an ionic polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) sensor acts as a touch controller to operate a commercial robotic manipulator. The environment consisted of five parts: an ionic PDMS, a microcontroller unit (MCU), a commercial robot gripper, a robot arm, and a main controller (personal computer). The ionic PDMS is a touchpad to control the robot arm position and the finger position of the gripper. The MCU received four voltages from the corner of the sensor. These voltages are used to estimate the presence and location of touch using the algorithm and find out the operator's command. The robot arm was controlled through ROS using libraries (1), and the operator commanded the finger position of the gripper by serial communication and libraries (2).

Figure 01. Triboresistive controller for manipulation of a robot hand and arm. (a) Block diagram describing communication between the triboresistive touch sensor and the robot. (b) The triboresistive touch sensor was attached to the back of a hand. Each corner of the ionic PDMS was connected with wires to a microcontroller unit (MCU). Scale bar: 2 cm. (c) The robot was connected to the sensor to demonstrate precise control of the robot’s posi-tion, orientation, gripping, and releasing. Scale bar: 8 cm. (d) Demonstration of control of the robot with various motions including “Move down” (i), “Grip” (ii), “Move up” (iii), “Next page” (iv), “Decrease roll” (v), “Release” (vi), etc. Scale bar: 8 cm.

Figure 02. Pre-programmed functions for control of a robot arm. Each segment of the ionic PDMS corresponded to a different function.

Figure 03. Touch position estimation flowchart. The algorithm was designed to estimate a touch position on a triboresistive touch sensor. Voltages generated at each corner of the triboresistive touch sensor are received in each loop. If at least one voltage is larger than a value of threshold, the algorithm starts to record peak of voltages during programmed loop cycles. The algorithm stops recording if all generated voltages are less than the value of threshold during the loop cycles. By comparing values of the recorded peak voltages, the algorithm estimates normalized distances, α and β. Sign of generated voltages is also used to distinguish recording start and end moments.

Figure 04. Voltages induced at each corner of the ionic PDMS. Induced voltages according to touch position and sixteen corresponding commands.

Figure 05. Touch position and corresponding sensing data during the robot mission. The triboresistive touch sensor perceived touch, detach, and holding movements and expressed “Move down,” “Grip,” “Move up,” “Next page,” “Roll (+),” “Release,” and so on.
